# Career Goals

## Career Background

A summary of the full career arc, for context when writing case studies and positioning content.

- **Williams-Sonoma / Google** — Started in production and visual design. Realized the work I wanted to do was software: products that persist, evolve, and generate ongoing value for users — not static artifacts with a short shelf life.
- **First startup** (~7 months) — Retail/consumer space. Small team, broad scope. The startup shut down, but it confirmed I thrived in environments where I could move fast, have real impact, and try new things.
- **Vendus** (Oct 2014 – Jul 2016) — Co-founded a product design agency. Only designer. Developed a rapid prototyping process — discovery one day, working prototype the next — that became the core of how we won clients and built trust. Sold the agency after raising funding for a side project.
- **Sureify** — Built a B2B2C platform for the life insurance industry: web platform, mobile apps, and marketing websites spanning insurers, agents, and end clients. Did everything — product, web, and marketing design. Left because the domain wasn't a fit personally, and Sutter Hill Ventures recruited me to Eversight.
- **Eversight** — AI-powered software helping retailers optimize pricing. First exposure to top-tier VC-backed startup culture. Used rapid prototyping to develop new product lines, leading to 2 pilot programs that became part of the foundation for Eversight's acquisition by Instacart. Also learned how VCs operate and what makes SaaS business models work. Left for Observe — more interesting problems, closer offices.
- **Observe** — Joined as founding designer. Stayed through multiple product pivots, PMF discovery, major funding rounds, and acquisition. Left as Head of Design. Built the design team from zero. Key outcomes: 250% MAU increase, design that directly contributed to funding rounds.
- **Snowflake** — Current role. Applying the same skills at larger org scale — more stakeholders, established systems, broader cross-functional influence.


## Target Roles

Design work should speak to the following role types and company profiles.

### By role type
- **Staff / Principal Product Designer** at product-led B2B SaaS companies — deep IC track where technical domain expertise, systems thinking, and end-to-end ownership are the differentiators. Examples: Vercel, Linear, Figma, Hex, Mixpanel, Anthropic.
- **Senior Product Designer / Design Lead** embedded in a product team, shipping alongside engineering — owning a product area or workstream, not a whole org. Examples: Datadog, dbt Labs, Gusto, Rippling, Grafana Labs.
- **Design Manager (small team, player-coach)** at growth-stage startups — still close to the work with 2-4 reports, responsible for quality and craft as much as people. Only at companies where design has a genuine mandate and organizational support — not orgs where the role means fighting for a seat at the table. Examples: Chronosphere, Ashby, Weights & Biases, Monte Carlo.
- **Founding / First Design Hire → Team Builder** at early-stage startups with strong design values — the opportunity to build a team from scratch, but only where leadership genuinely champions design as a strategic function, not an afterthought.

### By domain type (where background is strongest)
The common thread is **tools that help people perform their jobs** — complex workflows, challenging domains, and products where deep understanding of the user's work context is the difference between something useful and something that gets in the way.

- AI infrastructure and ML platforms (Anyscale, Modal, Weights & Biases)
- Observability, DevOps, and data platforms (Datadog, Grafana Labs, dbt Labs, Fivetran, Chronosphere)
- Enterprise workflow and productivity tools (Gusto, Rippling, Retool, Vanta, Ashby)
- Complex B2B SaaS where the UX challenge is making powerful capabilities accessible (Figma, Notion, Linear, Hex, Postman)

### What these roles have in common
They all value designers who can operate in challenging, complex domains without hand-holding, make sound product decisions under ambiguity, and ship work that moves business metrics. The users are professionals doing demanding work, and the design challenge is making powerful tools feel intuitive without dumbing them down.


## Positioning

A designer who builds tools that help people do their jobs better — especially in challenging, complex domains where understanding the user's actual workflow is everything. The draw isn't technology specifically; it's the intellectual challenge of learning a complex domain deeply and finding the abstraction that makes it accessible. Technical domains happen to be where the most interesting problems are right now, but the skills transfer wherever the problem is hard.

Over a decade of experience spanning agency work, VC-backed startups, and enterprise software — including six years as founding designer and eventual Head of Design at Observe, a startup that went through multiple pivots to find PMF before being acquired. Not a designer who needs a polished brief; a designer who helps figure out what the brief should be.

The through-line: taking complex problem spaces and making them usable without oversimplifying. Respects the user's intelligence. Brings strategic clarity to messy situations. Goes deep into the domain — whether that's observability, retail pricing AI, data infrastructure, or something else entirely.

Prototyping is a core part of the practice — not as a handoff artifact, but as a thinking tool. Some of the most influential work has been prototypes that made an idea tangible fast enough to change a conversation.


## Key Strengths to Demonstrate

The portfolio should collectively prove these things:

- **Operates at the intersection of product strategy and design execution.** Not just a "strategic thinker" and not just a "pixel-pusher" — both, and knows when to shift between them.
- **Thrives in challenging complexity.** Can go deep on unfamiliar domains — data models, query languages, pricing algorithms, temporal data — and translate that understanding into interfaces that actually work. Doesn't need the domain explained; learns it and contributes to product direction from a place of genuine understanding.
- **Uses analogies as a design tool.** Finds patterns between complex unfamiliar domains and simpler familiar ones. The right analogy often unlocks the right interface.
- **Designs through ambiguity.** Navigating product pivots, finding PMF, making design decisions with incomplete information, and shipping anyway. Comfortable being wrong and iterating.
- **Cross-functional fluency.** Works shoulder-to-shoulder with engineers and holds their own in technical conversations. Earns trust from engineering-heavy orgs by speaking the language and respecting the constraints.
- **Connects design to business outcomes.** Doesn't just ship features — can draw a line from design decisions to metrics like activation, retention, MAU growth, and revenue. The Observe work contributed to 250% MAU increase and supported significant funding rounds. The Eversight prototyping work contributed to 2 pilot programs and the foundation for an acquisition.
- **Makes teams better.** Whether in an IC or leadership role, improves how the team works — better design-engineering collaboration, clearer decision-making, stronger shared understanding of the problem space. Has built a design team from zero and knows how to create an environment where designers do their best work.
- **Builds tools people actually use.** Deep empathy for professional users doing complex work. Designs from an understanding of real workflows, not idealized user journeys. The goal is always: does this make someone better at their job?
- **Prototyping as strategic thinking.** Rapid prototypes aren't deliverables — they're a shared language. Has used this approach to win clients, validate product lines, and accelerate alignment with stakeholders since the beginning of this career.


## What's Missing / Growth Areas

Gaps the portfolio should aim to fill:

- **A craft-forward piece.** Most existing work leads with strategy, process, and business impact. Need at least one case study where interaction design, visual polish, and micro-decisions are the star — showing the ability to get into the pixels and care deeply about the details.
- **Working within (not just creating) a design system.** The Observe work was largely building from zero, which is valuable but different from contributing to or evolving an established system. Relevant for roles at companies like Figma, Linear, or Vercel where the system already exists.
- **User research as a standalone strength.** Research shows up as a step in the process across existing work, but no piece positions it as a core competency. A case study that leads with a research insight driving a major product decision would round out the portfolio.
- **Collaboration at scale.** Most experience is in small startup contexts. For roles at larger companies, showing how to collaborate across multiple teams, navigate competing priorities, or influence product direction in a bigger org would strengthen positioning.
- **Non-observability domain.** The portfolio currently leans heavily into one vertical. A piece framed around the transferable challenge — complex workflow design, professional tools — rather than the specific domain would demonstrate that the skills transfer.
