# Slide Library

Reference spec for the 12 slide types used in Mark's interview decks. This is the shared vocabulary for the deck-translation skill, the case study template's `Feeds` annotations, and the Figma master slides.

## Context

- **Deck format:** Live, talking-paced portfolio walkthrough (45–60 min)
- **Audience:** Hiring managers and interview panels for Staff/Principal IC, Senior IC/Design Lead, or player-coach manager roles
- **Source:** Case studies in Project Library v2 (Notion), structured by the Case Study Template
- **Design principle:** The slide supports the voice. If the audience is reading, they're not listening.

## How to use this spec

Each slide type below is a *pattern*, not a strict layout. The spec defines:

- **Job:** what the slide is for in the narrative
- **Content:** what goes on it
- **Constraints:** what to avoid (usually: text density)
- **Fed by:** which case study template section(s) supply the content

The deck-translation skill reads this file to decide which slide types to use when converting a written case study into a presentation outline. The Figma master slide layouts implement these patterns visually.

## The 12 slide types

### 1. Title

**Job.** Open the case study. Set up what's about to be told.

**Content.**
- Project name
- One-liner (the descriptive, factual version)
- Mark's role + dates
- Optional: company logo or a single anchoring image

**Constraints.** No subtitle stack. No tagline + subtitle + dates + role all fighting for attention. One headline-weight thing, everything else demoted.

**Fed by.** One-Liner & Hook → use the One-Liner here. The Hook is the *spoken* opening, not on the slide.

---

### 2. Section divider

**Job.** Signal a beat change inside a single case study. Give the audience (and the speaker) a breath.

**Content.**
- One short phrase naming the next beat ("The tension," "What we shipped," "Where it went wrong")
- Optional: a numeral (01, 02, 03) for orienting

**Constraints.** No subtitle. No body text. This slide is whitespace with a label.

**Fed by.** Structural — generated by the deck-translation skill based on the case study's narrative arc, not by a Notion section.

---

### 3. Context

**Job.** Set the scene. What was the state of the world before Mark got involved? Why did this project matter?

**Content.**
- One sentence stating the business situation
- One supporting visual: org chart, market diagram, customer screenshot, before-state UI, photo of a whiteboard, etc.

**Constraints.** Resist the urge to explain the company, the market, *and* the team on one slide. Pick the one thing the audience needs to understand the stakes.

**Fed by.** Business Context. May produce 1–2 Context slides if the situation needs more than one beat.

---

### 4. Tension

**Job.** Name the hard problem. The thing that made this project worth telling a story about.

**Content.**
- One sentence stated as a tension, question, or contradiction
- Optional: a small visual that embodies the tension (two competing diagrams, a quote from a frustrated user, a screenshot of the broken thing)

**Constraints.** This is a single idea slide. Don't list three tensions. If there are three, that's three slides — or pick the strongest one.

**Fed by.** The Tension.

---

### 5. Artifact (full-bleed)

**Job.** Show the work. Let the artifact speak.

**Content.**
- One image filling the slide, or close to it
- Optional: a small caption (project phase, date, "v1," etc.)

**Constraints.** No annotations. No callouts. If the artifact needs explaining, it's an Artifact (annotated) slide instead. Use this when the artifact is the point and Mark's voice provides the context.

**Fed by.** Artifacts & References, Approach.

---

### 6. Artifact (annotated)

**Job.** Show the work *and* point at one or two specific things.

**Content.**
- One image, large but with margin for annotations
- 1–3 callouts pointing to specific elements
- Each callout is a short label (max ~10 words)

**Constraints.** Three callouts is the cap. If there are more things to say about the artifact, split it into multiple Artifact (annotated) slides — one per insight.

**Fed by.** Artifacts & References, Approach, sometimes Key Decisions.

---

### 7. Artifact comparison

**Job.** Show a fork or an evolution. Make a change visible.

**Content.**
- Two images side by side (or top/bottom), labeled clearly ("v1 / v2," "before / after," "option A / option B")
- Optional: a short label between or below the pair naming what changed

**Constraints.** Two artifacts only. If there are three options or three iterations, this is multiple comparison slides, not a triptych. The eye can compare two things; three becomes a chart.

**Fed by.** Key Decisions, Approach (when the approach involved iteration worth showing).

---

### 8. Decision

**Job.** Name a fork in the road. State what was chosen and what the alternatives were.

**Content.**
- The decision, stated as a sentence
- The alternatives that were considered (2–3, briefly)
- Optional: a one-line "why" — but Mark's voice carries most of this

**Constraints.** This is a structural slide, not a narrative one. The point is to *show* there was a choice, so the audience tracks the reasoning. Don't write the full rationale — that's the spoken part.

**Fed by.** Key Decisions. Typically pairs with an Artifact comparison slide.

---

### 9. Quote / insight

**Job.** Land a single idea. A pull quote from a user, a researcher, a teammate, or a stat that captures the moment.

**Content.**
- One quote or one stat, large
- Attribution if it's a quote
- Source if it's a stat

**Constraints.** One thing on the slide. If there are two great quotes, that's two slides, separated by a different slide type so they each get their own moment.

**Fed by.** Approach (research findings, team dynamics), Reflection (lessons learned), occasionally Outcome (a customer testimonial).

---

### 10. Outcome

**Job.** Show what changed. Make the impact concrete.

**Content.** One of:
- 1–3 metrics, large, with short labels (revenue impact, retention shift, adoption numbers)
- A before/after comparison (visual or numeric)
- A short list of what shipped

**Constraints.** Pick one of the three formats. Don't combine metrics + before/after + ship list on one slide — that's three slides.

**Fed by.** Outcome.

---

### 11. Reflection / closing

**Job.** End the case study. Give the audience the takeaway Mark wants them to leave with.

**Content.**
- One reflective statement: what was learned, what would be done differently, what changed in how Mark works
- Optional: a thematic image or a callback to the Title slide's anchor visual

**Constraints.** Not a recap. Not a summary slide. One idea, stated cleanly.

**Fed by.** Reflection.

---

### 12. Inter-case-study transition

**Job.** Bridge between case studies in a multi-project walkthrough. Reset the audience's attention.

**Content.**
- Minimal: a numeral (01 / 02 / 03), Mark's name or a thread phrase, or just whitespace
- Optional: a single sentence connecting the previous story to the next ("…and that pattern showed up again at the next company")

**Constraints.** This is a beat, not a slide with content. Resist the urge to summarize the previous case study or preview the next one — the audience just experienced one and is about to experience another.

**Fed by.** Structural — generated by the deck-translation skill based on the deck's overall arc.

## Slide type → Notion section mapping (reverse lookup)

For when working from a Notion case study and wondering "which slides does this section produce?"

| Notion section | Produces |
|---|---|
| One-Liner & Hook | Title (One-Liner). Hook is spoken, not slide content. |
| Business Context | Context (1–2) |
| Your Role & Leverage | Title slide metadata (role + dates). Mostly spoken. |
| The Tension | Tension |
| Approach | Artifact (full-bleed), Artifact (annotated), Quote/insight |
| Key Decisions | Decision + Artifact comparison (typically paired, one pair per decision) |
| Outcome | Outcome |
| Reflection | Reflection / closing, occasional Quote/insight |
| Artifacts & References | Artifact (full-bleed), Artifact (annotated) |
| Viable Positions | Not a direct slide source — informs *which* angle the deck takes, which shapes the whole sequence |

## Narrative arc reference (for the deck-translation skill)

A typical single case study, ordered:

1. Title
2. Context (1–2)
3. Tension
4. Section divider — "Approach" or similar
5. Artifact slides (full-bleed and annotated, mixed) — varies, 3–8 slides
6. Section divider — "Key decisions" or similar
7. Decision + Artifact comparison (one pair per decision, typically 2–3)
8. Section divider — "Outcome" or similar
9. Outcome
10. Reflection / closing

For a multi-case-study walkthrough, separate each case study with an Inter-case-study transition.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Process slides.** "Discovery → Research → Design → Ship" timelines are generic and text-heavy. Mark's process is shown through artifacts and decisions, not summarized as a flowchart.
- **Wall-of-text slides.** If a slide has more than ~25 words of body copy, it's competing with the speaker. Cut, split, or move to speaker notes.
- **Bullet lists of considerations or learnings.** Bullets read flat and don't earn their slide. Either commit to one idea (Quote/insight, Reflection) or show the artifact that embodies the considerations.
- **Recap slides.** A slide that summarizes what was just said wastes a beat. Use Section dividers for structure, not recaps.
- **Multi-purpose slides.** A slide that's trying to be Context + Tension + Approach all at once. Each slide has one job.
